About UsBack to Top

GetUp is an independent, grassroots community advocacy organisation which aims to build a more progressive Australia; an Australia that values environmental sustainability, economic fairness and social justice at its core.

GetUp empowers Australians from all walks of life to get involved and change the news, not just watch from the sidelines. Whether it’s getting in contact with an MP, signing a petition, attending an event or helping to get a television ad on the air, GetUp members take targeted, coordinated and strategic action to get real results. At 30 June 2014, GetUp membership had swelled to over 800,000 Australians.

Being an independent, not-for-profit organisation, GetUp does not accept donations from political parties or the Government, but instead relies on donations from its members to fund its work.

Climate Action

Climate change consistently rates as the #1 issue for GetUp members.
It is both urgent and important; a campaign we’ve been fighting to win for far too long considering we have such little time left in which to turn things around. This year the GetUp community not only helped deliver record numbers on the ground, we also started to pave the way for innovative tactics that have the potential to move millions of dollars out of climate destruction.

On November 17, a staggering 60,000 of us came together in 130 beautiful events across the country for GetUp’s National Day of Climate Action. We created news headlines across the country, with ABC, SBS, 9, 10, SKY, WIN, and 7 all covering the rallies. Our movement had in fact pulled off one of the largest rallies for climate action in Australia's history - earning the title of 'Abbott's Worst Enemy' for our work in climate activism.

The national rallies were attended by a broad cross-section of society: with religious figures, young activists and politicians standing shoulder to shoulder with firefighters and farmers. Supporters ranged from the very young to grandmothers and grandfathers in their nineties, rallying on behalf of future generations. Some of the most outstanding efforts were from GetUp members in regional areas, where hundreds of people gathered in small towns around the country.  Regional and country Australia's contribution was a tremendous part of what made the National Day of Climate Action so great. When our politicians turned on the evening news that day, one of the first things they heard was that GetUp members and residents in over 130 towns were demanding government do more to tackle climate change. And when politicians opened their newspapers the next day, they were forced to read the same.

Because of GetUp members in regional and country Australia, tens of thousands of Australians had the opportunity to participate locally in the National Day of Climate Action and contribute to such an important cause. Many of you told GetUp staff that although one person was registered as the organiser, there were lots of people and local organisations who passionately dedicated their time and energy to the organising effort.

All in all, the day garnered more than 140 media articles. Following the events, thousands of GetUp members contributed to fund the ongoing climate campaign. As the first significant mobilisation following the 2013 Election, GetUp members significantly changed the conversation about the Abbott Government’s climate policy and the public mood.

The diverse crowd of the national day of action also helped spawn the launch of Australians for Action, a mini-site which represents the broad community of notable Australians urging our government to take greater, stronger, more ambitious actions on climate change. Ambassadors include some of Australia’s most well-known and respected figures: actors, performers, scientists, TV personalities, business professionals; as well as everyday folk. To date, more than 100,000 people have signed on!

These efforts also paved the way for further climate work, as we turned to focus the Great Barrier Reef, stop coal seam gas and help consumers move their money away from organisations that profit from climate destruction.

People power evolves

Since the Abbott Government came to power in September, the denial and disrespect of climate science has been in the spotlight. Their very first official act was to abolish the Climate Commission, the independent body which advised the Government on climate change; along with removing key ministerial positions on climate change and science and axing hundreds of jobs at the CSIRO. People power has never been more needed.

Partnerships for change

Throughout the year, GetUp members also participated in initiatives from across the environmental movement. In early 2014, GetUp teamed up wth 350.org to urge corporations to do better on climate. GetUp members who are also customers of UniSuper emailed the Board and the National Tertiary Education Union, calling on UniSuper to measure and disclose its fossil fuel exposure, reduce its fossil fuel exposure across all portfolios, and ultimately divest and go fossil free.

The review would be headed up by Dick Warburton, a self-confessed climate sceptic, and panellists with significant connections to mining and industry. Thousands of GetUp members shared content on Facebook, highlighting the farcical appointments.

One day may this serve as an historical document, for our grandchildren to marvel at how hard we had to fight simply to evolve our way of doing business in order to protect the very planet that sustains us. Until then, we’ll keep working.

Great Barrier Reef

This was the year GetUp members made history in the effort to protect our Great Barrier Reef, with campaigning as big as the Reef itself.

When the Federal Government failed to protect the Reef against inappropriate development – approving the construction of the largest coal port terminal in the world inside the Reef’s heritage-listed area – GetUp members stepped up. First, thousands banded together to crowdfund two legal challenges to the approval of dredging and dumping inside the Reef. Then, we helped convince major banks that financing the destruction of the Reef was bad business. Finally, we exposed the utterly disgraceful record of the Indian-owned mining company our Government is so keen to entrust with the future of the Reef.

Five million tonnes sounds like a lot of anything, but when you’re talking about dredge spoil to be dumped on Australia’s most valuable environmental asset, it sounds downright ludicrous.

Yet on January 31, Environment Minister Greg Hunt approved the Abbot Point coal terminal expansion, which would mean massive dredging atnd dumping in the Great Barrier Reef World Heritage Area. The resulting coal pollution would cause four times as much pollution as New Zealand (yes, the entire country); and hundreds of coal ships would pass through the Reef every year.  And just to add to the good news, the company behind the project, Indian mining company Adani, has a proven history of environmental destruction, theft and bribery.

GetUp members had already spent the months in the lead-up to the decision lobbying hard with full-page ads, petitions, rallies outside the Minister’s office. So when the decision came down, we knew we had to pull out all the stops.

That’s when 17,500 GetUp members contributed over $300,000 to a citizens' Reef Fighting Fund. The cases are fighting the legality of the Minister’s approval in the courts on two grounds. First, that the decision to allow the dumping of dredge spoil in the World Heritage Area wasn't made in a lawful manner because the environmental assessment carried out was improper. Second, that Minister Hunt failed his obligation to protect the World Heritage Area when he approved the dredging.

We’re funding North Queensland Conservation Council to fight the dumping approval, and Mackay Conservation Group to fight the dredging approval. The Environmental Defenders Office of Queensland will be the lawyers in both cases.

These groups have been fighting to protect our environments on the ground for decades. They have the knowledge, experience and determination required to take these historic cases to the court. We’re proud to support these two groups.

Both developers and government now know they simply can't treat the environment with such blatant disregard, and must now factor litigation into any project known to be environmentally unsound.

Another important way GetUp members fought back to protect the Reef was through undermining the ability of mining companies to attract the investment they needed for the project go ahead.

It started with convincing Deutsche Bank to publicly walk away from the project. GetUp members worked internationally with our German counterpart, Campact, flooding the bank’s Facebook and Twitter accounts and petitioning them directly. The pressure was finally too much when GetUp members chipped in and ran a full page ad in the Financial Times of Europe.  This win was closely followed by two more: with Barclays and Royal Bank of Scotland also declaring they wouldn’t back the project after pressure from Melbourne. This has been a game-changer, and investment banks know they now have to take into account potential backlash from citizens and consumers when they invest in irresponsible projects.

Finally, GetUp members made a video explaining the history of Adani -- the Indian-owned mining company with an atrocious human rights and environmental record. It went viral online and was noticed by potential investors across the world.

Then, suddenly, the video disappeared. It appeared that an individual whom has a business relationship with Adani set up a fake website, uploaded our video, and launched a copyright action claiming our video as their own. That means the video was taken down just days after Adani announced it was looking for banks to fund their project.

GetUp worked with lawyers and YouTube to get the video back online. But seeing as we’d hit a nerve, we decided to supercharge the message. GetUp members donated to a sophisticated online, targeted advertising campaign to make sure every potential investor in Adani saw the video. Over four thousand people donated, and the video is being seen by hundreds of thousands across the world.

We then re-uploaded the video to Facebook, and the post has reached over 1.4 million people; and the resulting media coverage drove interest further. It was the most popular story on The Sydney Morning Herald website for much of the day. That night GetUp National Director Sam McLean featured on The Project, and future media investigations would lead to damning stories about Adani.

But we have been here before and we know that this is far from over. Adani and all the others who are happy to profit from the reef’s destruction will not give up without a fight, so the GetUp movement must be ready for whatever comes next in order to save one of Australia’s national treasures.

ABC

GetUp members all over the country are deeply passionate about protecting the ongoing funding for our iconic national broadcaster, and have proved it in actions over many years. To date, the petition to keep the ABC free of charge, politically independent, fair and balanced has grown to over a quarter of a million signatures. But over the course of this year, GetUp members have shown their real passion for the national broadcaster by showing up when it matters, and where it counts.

GBefore the 2013 Election, Tony Abbott made a commitment to make no cuts to the ABC or SBS. Yet, in the face of widely reported rumours that drastic cuts were coming for Australia's national broadcaster in the 2014-15 budget, GetUp members sprung into action for a fierce campaign of public pressure.

In the lead-up to the budget, GetUp members mobilised in a broad and brave campaign, and our efforts paid off. In the 2014-15 budget, the new Abbott Government may have broken their election promise and made cuts to public broadcasting –  axing the Australia Network and cutting funds for ABC and SBS by 1%. Yet, just weeks before budget night, commentators and Coalition members were talking of cuts up to ten times that amount.

Without the extraordinary efforts of GetUp members to intervene in the key decision making period and demonstrating the palpable backlash, the cuts may have been much worse.

We have a proud history of fighting for the ABC, so what did we do this time?

GetUp members targeted key decision makers where they couldn't avoid our messages. We put ads in the paper, and produced a widely shared video featuring Tony Abbott's own words committing to no cuts to the ABC or SBS. Communications Minister Malcolm Turnbull and Prime Minister Tony Abbott both faced giant crowd-funded billboards on arterial roads in their electorate.

GetUp members will continue to rally for the ABC – standing up for fair and balanced reporting, and for the ABC's funding. It's no coincidence that "save our ABC" bumper stickers and the Bananas in Pyjamas are still seen all over Australia!  

Partnerships for change

GetUp members visited their representatives in electorates around the country, marching into electoral offices to deliver copies of our quarter of a million-strong petition. These events were grassroots-organised, by 66 everyday GetUp members who put their hands up to be ABC Champions. They were charged with organising the events logistics, recruiting fellow GetUp members to attend and delivery of the physical petition. In total, these 66 champions we’re able to organise and recruit 1,913 GetUp members to be part of petition delivery events! Prominent figures like Play School’s Benita Collings and journalist Jonathan Holmes joined us in urging our leaders not to undermine the ABC’s incredible that serve so many Australians from the cradle to the grave.

Together, we also formed human signs in the shape of the ABC logo at events in Sydney and Melbourne. Thousands of event attendees wrote their messages to hand-deliver to our politicians in Canberra. Then, on Budget Day, we erected a message that was impossible to miss: a  20-metre tall ABC logo plastered on Parliament Lawns, with the messages from everyday Australians for our politicians, media and fellow citizens to see. Hands off our ABC!

Refugees

The 2013 Federal Election was the impetus for significant changes in refugee policy in 2013/2014. Much of the GetUp movement’s campaign efforts focussed around exposing the abhorrent nature of Australia’s offshore processing policy, in response to a government determined to control any information about Australian-run detention centres.

And when asylum-seeker Reza Berati died in our care on Manus Island, more than 20,000 Australians came together to light a candle at more than 750 candlelight vigils around the country.

This year our movement was forced to adapt to increasingly harsher measures by political parties who attempted to appear tough against people smugglers. On 19 July 2013, the then-Prime Minister Kevin Rudd announced the “PNG solution” – billed as a deterrence strategy that would see all asylum seekers who arrived by boat sent to Papua New Guinea. Anyone who arrived after that date would be refused protection in Australia, even if found to be a refugee.

GetUp members voiced their concern about the new so-called ‘hardline’ measures to their MPs, as part of an ongoing campaign to expose the completely inadequate standard of care provided to asylum seekers in detention centres on Manus Island and Nauru.

In the lead up to the federal election, more than 1,000 GetUp members fundraised to air television ads in targeted electorates featuring Lillian, whose Jewish family were welcomed into Australia after fleeing Nazi-occupied Germany during the Second World War.

Eleven days after winning the election, the Abbott Coalition Government implemented one of their key plans to “stop the boats” with the establishment of ‘Operation Sovereign Borders’. This coincided with a media crackdown by the Immigration Minister Scott Morrison, who refused to provide the public information on what he declared were “operational matters”. The Abbott Government also seemed to receive a helping hand in keeping the media at arm’s length: the Nauruan government increased the cost of a journalist visa to a staggering $8,000!

Citizen-funded journalism

In response, more than 4,700 GetUp members funded a citizens’ journalism campaign, to help tell the stories the Abbott Government was so keen to suppress. GetUp established an independent panel to provide these member-funded grants to journalists, made of some of the country’s most respected and experienced journalists, media producers and executives: including Wendy Harmer, Eric Beecher, Monica Attard, Barat Ali Batoor and John Birmingham.

Vigils for Reza Berati

On February 17, 2014, we witnessed what many hoped would be a turning point for our government’s stance on asylum seekers. Violence broke out inside the Manus Island detention centres and 23 year-old Iranian asylum seeker, Reza Berati, was killed. Just three days after news broke of Reza’s death, more than 20,000 Australians came together at more than 750 candlelight vigils around the country. Vigils in capital cities were organised with the invaluable support of groups such as Welcome to Australia, the First Home Project, the Asylum Seeker Resource Centre, Amnesty International and the Refugee Action Coalition. The resulting media coverage was high-profile and ongoing, as was the conversation on social media.

Can Labor Party Policy be Changed?

On 17 June 2014, GetUp members stood alongside federal Labor MPs Melissa Parke and Anna Burke in calling for a change in the ALP’s policy on asylum seekers. Sadly the Parke and Burke motion was defeated in the party room. Yet 5,993 GetUp members reached out to tell their Labor MPs they want to see them change course on asylum seeker policy.

Coal Seam Gas

Coal seam gas has been another important campaign for the GetUp movement. This year we focused on a critical campaign to protect the Pilliga forest, a vast expanse of bushland, located between Narrabri and Coonabarabran in western NSW, from becoming an industrial coal seam gas field.

After years of insisting CSG projects pose no threat to our water supply, mining company Santos was caught out poisoning an aquifer in the Pilliga Forest. The aquifer is now filled with uranium at 20 times the safe drinking water levels, and has elevated levels of arsenic, lead, aluminium, nickel, barium and boron.

The official consequences for Santos were laughable: a $1500 slap on the wrist. And unbelievably, two days after the fine was issued, the NSW State Government signed an agreement with Santos to fast track additional CSG development in the area. Drilling in the Pilliga is seen as the trojan horse for the spread of industrial gas fields across the NSW North West, including some of the best food producing lands in Australia, the Liverpool Plains.

The mining company Santos are drilling in the Pilliga, with plans to put forward a production application for an alarming 850 wells in the Pilliga to the Federal and State Governments in mid 2014.

After news broke that Santos had contaminated an aquifer with heavy metals including uranium in the Pilliga State Forest, the response was immediate.

43,448 GetUp members from across Australia rallied behind the local community, signing a petition to Santos CEO David Knox calling on him to drop the Narribri CSG project.

161 people then used their power as Santos shareholders to present the petition to the company, in a targeted campaign to stop Santos from the inside out. The shareholder resolution was put together by The Wilderness Society, based on extensive scientific research into the damage Santos has already caused, and the threat their proposed expansion poses to a crucial recharge area of the Great Artesian Basin. Together, we forced a shareholder resolution to abandon the Narribri Gas Project to be put on the agenda of the Santos Annual General Meeting (AGM) for a vote.

Dozens of farmers and Traditional Owners made the journey from northwest NSW to Adelaide for Santos AGM. The stories they told were incredible. As The Australian reported, "for nearly two hours of a four hour meeting, the motion was debated, giving the group of farmers and environmentalists plenty of time to show the board and shareholders the extent of their opposition."

Gomeroi Traditional Owner Deborah Briggs travelled to the AGM to speak for her country and Elders, "I've seen the damage that Santos coal seam gas has done to the Pilliga, the heart of our Gomeroi Nation. Our Elders would walk to the end of the Earth to protect the Pilliga, and I've carried their voices to the Santos AGM to speak against the Narrabri Gas Project."

In the end the resolution got smashed, but all the media coverage was about the protest of people at the meeting. Not long after that, The Australian reported the people’s backlash against the project had stripped $600 million from the value of Santos’ Narribri Gas Project.

In an unambiguous display of community opposition to Santos’ presence in the Narribri, GetUp members also put in an extraordinary fundraising effort to run ads in national newspapers. In just 48 hours, 2260 donors chipped in. The response was so strong our media buyer was not only able to place the ad in The Financial Review, but secured a full page ad in The Australian.

The ad featured farmers whose livelihoods could be under threat from another coal seam gas groundwater contamination. Thank you to all those who took time out during harvest to help make the ad happen. The dynamic fundraising effort helped us reach over half a million Australians. Even better, the ad sparked national media attention, generating headlines like 'Santos told: Your gas is on the nose.'

A special thank you for the relentless work of the Northwest Alliance, The Wilderness Society, Lock The Gate and all other groups involved in this fight. Without your dedication, none of this would be possible.

Santos' CEO and executives now know they have to justify their actions to shareholders and to the public. No longer will they be allowed operate as they like, treating the land they use with contempt and not expect to face the consequences.

2013 Federal Election

As the largest independent political movement in Australia, GetUp is in a unique position to hold all parties accountable at election time and shine a spotlight on the issues that matter, but which don’t always get the media attention they deserve.

We knew it was going to be a tough and important contest, with a real risk of one party gaining not only Government, but rule of the Senate as well. So we rolled out our most ambitious election program ever this year -- powered by 17,000 donors and more than 6,000 grassroots volunteers around the country.

Together, we reached more than 3 million Australians with a combination of innovative digital tactics, cut-through television ads and good old fashioned pounding of the pavement.

Every election is important – but this year we knew there was a very real risk of one conservative party winning not only government, but sole control of the Senate as well. That was high stakes for democracy and many of the issues GetUp members care about most, so we crafted our most ambitious plan yet to empower the voices of ordinary Australians to make a difference.

It started with GetUp members gathering in 300 grassroots GetTogether meetings around the country, to discuss the issues and help set our movement’s election agenda. We then followed up with a national online survey of GetUp members, to reinforce our movement’s mandate to go big and campaign for our top priorities. The result: our most ambitious election agenda ever.

Enrolment

At GetUp we care about the issues, and we care about democracy itself; the right and responsibility of every Australian to contribute and have a say. So we made sure our enrol to vote campaign was a game-changer. Together we pioneered online enrolment technology, and fought to make it legal. The Australian Electoral Commission went from opposing the very idea of online enrolment to copying GetUp’s technology, which in this election was used by 83 per cent of new voters!

We went into this election knowing that 1.4 million people eligible to vote were missing from the electoral roll, including 493,000 under the age of 24.

We knew it would take innovative tactics to reach those less likely to be politically engaged -- so we launched a groundbreaking Facebook app, initiated neighbour-to-neighbour enrolment drives and made headlines with our national Enrol for Gold contest, which encouraged young people to register to vote for a chance to win a share of $40,000 in gold bullion prize money.

All up, voter enrolment went up 30% this election. That includes 162,000 people who would have been disenfranchised by the early closure of the roll, had GetUp not previously fought and won to have these Howard-era laws struck down by the High Court.

Holding all the parties accountable

As an independent movement, GetUp is political but not partisan. That means at election time, we’re in a unique position to give an unflinching assessment of where the parties really stand on the issues of the day. So we asked Labor, Liberal, the Greens, Palmer United Party and other parties to answer real questions on policy, then put the results into a handout that was easy to understand at a glance.

All up, more than 6,000 volunteers handed out 2.4 million scorecards to voters at election booths around the country -- giving information voters could use education, health, the environment, refugees, public broadcasting and penalty rates. Thousands more voters accessed the scorecard information online.

We also hosted live multi-party Q&A candidate forums in Town Halls across the country, and online with Kevin Rudd and Christine Milne (Tony Abbott declined our invitation). All up, GetUp members organised 10 candidate forums in key electorates, which were attended by 66 candidates who answered questions directly their local constituents.

Cutting through the noise to protect democracy

First up, we knew one party was poised to win not only government, but control of the Senate. The last time this happened we got the Northern Territory intervention with minimal debate, Australian troops were sent to Iraq on faulty intelligence and WorkChoices became law of the land.  

Yet research has shown repeatedly that many Australians just don’t understand the Senate or its importance; so we created “Game of Seats”, a cheeky video explaining how the Senate works. This parody ad was viewed more than 100,000 times online alone.

We also took on powerful interests when we called out the Murdoch press for their ridiculously biased election coverage. Our ad, reminiscing about the days when you could pick up a newspaper and get, well, news – openly accused the Murdoch media of serving their readers “misleading crap”. The major commercial networks refused to air it, which only sent its online reach soaring to over 800,000 views! These were just two standout videos among many that helped reshape and reclaim the political debate for the people. No wonder Google named GetUp the top political channel online, far ahead of Labor and Liberal in reach.

Rallies on the issues that matter

Lastly, GetUp members took to the streets in key moments to stand, walk and even dress up for the issues we care about. In Brisbane, over 3,000 people rallied to protect the Great Barrier Reef. In Canberra, we came out in support of renewable energy, and in Melbourne we held rallies for climate action. We also threw our weight behind campaigns for the landmark Gonski education reforms.

Regardless of what GetUp members think about the political outcome of the election, we can be proud that our movement stepped up to become more active, energised and powerful than ever.

Democracy

On July 2, 2013 the Queensland Government set out to ‘solve’ a problem that doesn’t really exist: voter fraud. Their plan: to require people to prove their identity. Not just when they enrol, but every time they vote.  Not only that, but they decided to allow a whole lot more (secret) money into politics, and make the head of the corruption watchdog a party-appointed position.

ournalists and academics said the new voter ID laws were simply about silencing the people less likely to vote conservative. The proposed changes were likely to silence up to two thirds of Indigenous Australians who do not have a driver’s license; along with the elderly, young, disabled, homeless and those who move house frequently.

The Queensland Government were also planning to make other backwards changes to State legislation, allowing in:

• Floods of cash

They proposed scrapping Queensland's donation caps of $5,300 to a party and $2,200 per candidate, throwing open the floodgates to big companies and big money.

• Secret Money

They wanted to allow up to $1.1 million to be given secretly to MPs without being declared.

• Raiding the Public Kitty

A new stack of taxpayer money – likely to be in the millions millions – was proposed. This would flow to the major parties, with the majority going to the party in power, helping to keep them there.

Finally the Queensland Government said they would change the structure of the corruption watchdog. Instead of being a bi-partisan decision, the head of the Crime and Misconduct Commission would now be picked by the Government alone. The Commission would also be instructed to give less priority to uncovering political corruption.

In response to this attack on integrity, thousands of Queensland GetUp members got loud, coordinated and creative.

  • • We chipped in to run hard-hitting ads that exposed the plan in Queensland's most read paper, The Courier-Mail.

  • • We wrote to our local QLD newspapers and our MPs; 162 QLD GetUp members even made submissions to the parliamentary committee tasked with reviewing the legislation in January.

  • • GetUp members appeared in a video that was seen by more than 15,000 people about why voter suppression mattered, and the importance of protecting the integrity of elections.

  • • We delivered a petition to the QLD Attorney General, and put a porcelain throne on the lawn outside his office and told him to flush toilet paper ballot papers – not real votes! The next day members delivered the petition to the Premier and took a pinata full of chocolate votes to the Premier’s office, asking him to take a stick to the chocolate-filled ballot box, not real ones. And in May, GetUp members delivered the petition to the Parliament – feeding a ballot box into a wood chipper.

We didn’t stop there. In the week before the vote GetUp members wrote to their MPs to let them know that a personalised webpage had been built for them, which would show how they voted on the laws. In addition, GetUp members advised that every time a voter searched for their MP on Google, the link to that MP's "Highest Bidder" webpage would be front and centre!

Outcome

Despite the strong majority of the governing Liberal National Party pushing these laws, and lack of any watchdog Senate in QLD state parliament, important concessions were still won.

  • • The final laws enable people who couldn’t meet ID requirements on election day to still cast a vote by signing a declaration saying they are who they claim to be, ensuring tens of thousands of Queenslanders will still be able to have their say at elections.

  • • The Labor Party, Katter Party, Palmer Party and key independents voted against the changes, and the Opposition has committed to reversing them once in Government.

Following the passage of the laws the Assistant Health Minister and Member for Stafford, Dr Chris Davis, resigned in protest.  When whispers surfaced that the QLD Premier might leave the people of Stafford without a representative for 10 months to prevent another bruising by-election, more than 10,000 GetUp members added their name to a petition asking the Premier to call a by-election.  On the morning the petition was due to be delivered (less than an hour after a press release went out about the delivery) the Premier announced a date for the by-election!

Save Our Forests

On June 24, after just seven minutes of deliberation, the World Heritage Committee rejected Prime Minister Tony Abbott's submission to have Tasmania's ancient forests stripped of their protection status and opened to logging.

This was a campaign emphatically won by people power, and GetUp members were proud to play our part alongside other champions for the Australian environment. Together, we delivered a 368,000-strong petition, flooded the Senate enquiry with submissions, and bought billboards all over Doha, Qatar urging the protection of Tasmania’s ancient forests.

It was the first time a country had applied to have a site of existing world heritage value de-listed. Thankfully, the World Heritage Committee unanimously rejected Prime Minister Tony Abbott's submission to have Tasmania's ancient forests stripped of their protection status and opened to logging.

It took the 21 delegate World Heritage Committee less than seven minutes to reach their decision, stating that:

The attack on our forests had been underhanded and deceitful. Although Abbott's submission to the World Heritage Committee was contradicted by the Department of Environment, other right-wing forces tried every tired trick. The right-wing Institute of Public Affairs even went so far as setting up a fake environment group, misleadingly named the Australian Environment Foundation, with the intention of confusing the Committee by pretending to represent the Australian environment movement.

But luckily, the story didn't end there.

Our movement mobilised and fought hard to defend the forests – and a genuine, massive, heartfelt public response is something the forces of greed and self-interest just can't fake. Alongside some of the world's leading environment groups, we put our names to an epic 368,869 person petition to protect Tasmania's forests. Our friends at the Australian Conservation Foundation took your voices straight to the hands of the World Heritage Committee, delivering the joint petition to them on the doorsteps of the conference centre in Doha

When our Senate launched an official inquiry into the proposal in March, GetUp members knew exactly what to do. Every morning as the delegates of the World Heritage Committee left their hotel to resume negotiations their hearts and minds were filled with images of Tasmania's forests and our message: these forests are outstanding, and the people of Australia want them to remain protected.

At the very last minute, GetUp member donations made it possible to support the Environment Tasmania-led delegation of World Heritage experts to go to Doha to be a voice for the forests’ protection at the official conference.

This was an incredible campaign, one that illustrated the strength of our movement when we come together and fight to protect the issues we care about.

Our Culture

Organisational culture is a system of shared values, ideas, beliefs, behaviours and stories. It determines how we work together and our capacity to make change. By articulating our culture, we commit to holding ourselves and each other to the values that strengthen us as a team. Each of them is crucial to allowing us to better serve our movement for a more progressive Australia.

Our People

1. We are member focused

We put GetUp members at the centre of all campaign strategies and tactics – they are instrumental to any campaign victory. We take our responsibility as the stewards of members’ time and money very seriously. We are open with and accountable to our members. We are GetUp members.

2. We are powered by passion

We are lifelong members of the progressive movement. We are powered by a deep and abiding passion for progressive change, for resisting conservative forces, and for fighting injustice and inequality.

3. We strive for impact and excellence

Our success is measured not by number of members, Facebook likes, emails opened or dollars coming in. It is measured by whether we are influencing discourses, debates and decision makers.

4. We derive strength from diversity

A diverse team, membership and movement makes us better at seeing and fighting injustice. Our impacts are strongest when informed by diverse perspectives.

5. We are one piece of the puzzle

GetUp is one cog in the progressive movement wheel. We are humbled by the knowledge and talent of other progressive groups, and recognise them as an invaluable part of everything we do.

6. We are audacious

Audacity is our standard practice. While we manage for risk, we never accept ‘it can't be done’ or ‘that's impossible’.

7. We are nimble

We always have our ear to the ground to identify opportunities to be impactful, and seize key moments as they arise. Our rapid response is always weighed with accuracy and quality, a balance we try to get right by focusing on impact.

8. We experiment, evaluate and evolve

We are always innovating and experimenting. We constantly scrutinise our work in terms of impact and reflect critically on what we could be doing better.

9. We stand and fall together

We are a team and we are greater than the sum of our parts. We depend on each other's talents, expertise and passions to achieve excellence in all that we do.
